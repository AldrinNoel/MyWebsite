Chidhood

     When I was a kid I never really understood how the world worked, nothing seemed wrong to me, I thought “things just happened”.1 Like there’s no permanent consequences to the things you do.2 Well, the thing is, one choice can actually alter the course of someone’s life.3 No matter what it is, it can either be a positive or a negative experience.4 And that realization didn’t hit me until later in life.5 I was born on December 24 of 2005, I was the youngest of three siblings.6 My mother and father’s hometown are both located in Mindanao.7 I remember having to go on a 3 day road trip just to visit my grandmother and grandfather, I didn't know it at that moment, I was having the best time of my life.8 Because of all those experiences, I developed a sense of taste for road trips.9 With the smell of trees and green fields breeze rushing through the windows of the car with music that makes you feel so nostalgic.10 The food you eat during brunch or a midnight snack that’s either a home cooked meal or from restaurants or fast foods that we passed.11 The excitement you feel when there’s a great view like the ocean, forest, and mountains during sunsets, mornings or even late at night.12 These moments felt like an escape from reality, a world where freedom from earthly concepts truly exist.13 That’s why I’ll probably go on a lot more adventures when I earn my work money.14 My childhood was filled with great memories.15 I was the kid who didn’t care about what kind of person you were, if you didn’t do anything wrong with me then there’s no reason for me not to talk to you.16 That’s why I always had a problem with adults, they always seem to nitpick what’s wrong about a kid and what’s so great about them.17 They’re literal kids, what’s wrong if they move, act, and have fun differently from the other kids.18 When I was in 5th grade I changed schools.19 I didn’t know by then that those actions were actually the norm.20 Still little by little, those condescending or confidence boosting words, pierced my mentality, chaining my inner peace.21

Teenage Years

     In high school, as if the word itself wasn’t bad enough, I was struck by the most dreadful experience of my life.1 Puberty hit me like a storm I never signed up for.2 I was suddenly plagued with insecurities from head to toe.3 I began to think that being mature, confident, and attractive during teenage years should honestly be considered a crime.4 It was during this confusing time that I was introduced to cliques.5 I didn’t know what to expect or who I was supposed to belong to.6 My teenage years felt like a long tunnel where I couldn’t find the light.7 I kept trying to figure out who I was supposed to be.8 I slowly felt disconnected from the people I genuinely cared about.9 I lost my spark without even noticing it slip away.10 When I was younger, I could talk to anyone without hesitation.11 I could become friends with every classmate like it was the easiest thing in the world.12 I would jump from friend group to friend group just to start a conversation.13 And then the pandemic happened, and that small loneliness I felt became something bigger.14 I already felt alone, but now I became more alone.15 Two years of being separated from normal teenage life pushed me into being chronically online.16 I spent hours scrolling through every social media platform I could escape into.17 Somehow, in all that digital noise, I slowly learned how to reflect on myself.18 I started discovering who I wanted to become, even if it was only little by little.19 I began drawing out of boredom, and even as a self-taught artist, it led me to win a few digital poster-making competitions.20 It was only school-based so it wasn’t that special, but it meant something to me.21 And even though my family and I were together 24/7 during the lockdown, I felt more distant from them than ever before.22 Maybe it was the teenage phase, maybe it was something else, but it made me want to become so rich that I could live like a potato in my remaining years.23

College Years

     Pre-college became the shift from being locked inside our rooms to actually facing people again.1 It marked the beginning of my senior high school life.2 I suddenly felt like the world was giving me a new chance.3 I joined clubs, varsity groups, and even chorale just to use whatever talents I had.4 I pushed myself into spaces I used to be scared of.5 I even became president of the English club and handled events I never imagined leading.6 I slowly changed from someone who struggled to hold a simple conversation into someone who made friends easily.7 It felt refreshing, almost like a rebirth.8 I was overjoyed by the new version of myself.9 But joy, as usual, didn’t last as long as I hoped.10 There was still this part of me that resisted acceptance.11 It was heavy, like a weight I didn’t want to admit existed.12 College came, and I followed in my sister's footsteps by choosing UE.13 With the help of a friend and some talent I barely acknowledged, I earned a scholarship.14 It felt like I was finally getting the college experience I used to dream about.15 But with dreams came pressure I wasn’t prepared for.16 I would wake up in the middle of the night because of stress I didn’t know how to handle.17 And then came the hardest part, my entire friend group from SHS transferred back to our hometown.18 It felt like the world took away my safety net in one quick move.19 A part of the pressure disappeared, but a different kind of pressure grew in its place.20 I wanted to be perfect because I feared that even one wrong decision would hurt not only me but also the people who trusted me.21 It became too heavy at times, but my scholarship eased the burden on my family.22 And despite everything, I still managed to hang out with new friends and balance the chaos that came with college life.23
